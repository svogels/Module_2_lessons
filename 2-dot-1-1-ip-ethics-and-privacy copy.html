What is Copyright Law?

Copyright law protects creators and encourages innovation.

  • Creates incentives for people to invest time, talent, and resources in creating new material
  • Particularly benefits cultural and educational content
  • In Australia: governed by the Copyright Act 1968 (Cth)
  • Federal legislation applying throughout all of Australia

Source: ACC, Introduction to copyright in Australia

Using websites and copyright

  • In most cases a website will have the following symbol © 2015 SYDNEY DANCE COMPANY
  • This indicates they own the copyright or have permission or a license to use the material on their website
  • Some websites have detailed copyright information, which is located at the bottom of the website. These have a hyperlink to another page to the conditions to use the website including: Conditions of Use, Terms of Use, Terms & Conditions, Disclaimer

Copyright & Intellectual Property Obligations when downloading from the internet

Common Misconception: Many believe the internet is 'public domain' with no copyright protection. This is NOT true.

Key Points:

  • Copyright protection exists on most creative content online:
    • Printed forms
    • Music
    • Graphics and illustrations
    • Photos and videos
  • Just because content is online doesn't mean it's free to use
  • You must respect IP rights and copyright when downloading from the internet

Resource: The Australian Copyright Council has published a detailed guide:

"Internet: Copying and Downloading"

Available at: www.copyright.org.au

What is Intellectual Property (IP)?

Definition: Ownership of an idea or design by the person or organisation that created it.

4 Main Types of Intellectual Property:

Patents

  • Legal right to exclude others from making, using, or selling an invention
  • Limited time period of protection
  • Requires publishing details of the invention

Trademarks

  • Recognizable sign, design, or expression
  • Identifies products or services from a specific source
  • Distinguishes them from competitors
  • Owner can be an individual, business, or legal entity

Copyright

  • Exclusive right to copy, distribute, adapt, display, and perform creative work
  • Usually for a limited time
  • Covers literary, artistic, educational, or musical forms

Trade Secrets

  • Includes formulas, practices, processes, designs, or information compilations
  • Has economic value because it's not generally known
  • Owner must take reasonable measures to keep it secret
  • Protects against unauthorized disclosure
  • Also called "confidential information" in some jurisdictions

IP Australia: The government agency that administers IP rights and legislation.

Introduction to Copyright in Australia

  • Copyright Act 1968 (Cth) regulates copyright in Australia
  • Covers: original literary, dramatic, musical and artistic works
  • Australian Copyright Council (ACC): not-for-profit organization promoting understanding of copyright law (established 1968)

Key Resources:

  • 📄 Copyright Law Fact Sheet
  • 🔗 More ACC fact sheets

Government Department: Department of Infrastructure, Transport, Regional Development and Communications

⚠️ Important thing to note:

  • Australian copyright law only applies within Australian borders
  • Outside Australia: work is subject to that country's copyright laws
  • International protection: Treaties like the Berne Convention and Universal Copyright Convention establish minimum legal requirements globally

What is Privacy?

Privacy is a fundamental human right that underpins freedom of association, thought, expression, and protection from discrimination.

Privacy includes the right to:

  • Be free from interference and intrusion
  • Associate freely with whom you want
  • Control who can see or use information about you

Three types of privacy:

  • Physical privacy: E.g., airport security checks, bodily samples for medical reasons
  • Surveillance: When identity can't be proved or information isn't recorded
  • Information privacy: How your personal information is handled

What is Information Privacy?

  • Protects information about who we are, what we do, and what we believe

Privacy Act 1988:

  • Covers personal information handling by:
    • Australian Government agencies
    • Organisations with annual turnover > $3 million
    • Some other organisations
  • Doesn't specifically cover surveillance (but may apply in some situations)

Regulator: Office of the Australian Information Commissioner (OAIC)

🔗 Learn more about privacy in Australia

⚠️ Important thing to note:

  • Privacy rights are not absolute
  • Sometimes other concerns take priority: safety, interests of justice
  • Strict rules apply when privacy is limited

Privacy in Organisations

Australian Privacy Principles (APPs): The cornerstone of privacy protection in the Privacy Act 1988

Who must follow APPs? Any organisation or agency the Privacy Act covers

13 Australian Privacy Principles govern:

  • Collection, use and disclosure of personal information
  • Organisation/agency governance and accountability
  • Integrity and correction of personal information
  • Individual rights to access their personal information

Why principles-based?

  • Flexible: organisations can tailor practices to their business models
  • Technology neutral: adapts to changing technologies
  • Accommodates diverse needs of individuals

Consequences: Breaching an APP = "interference with privacy" → regulatory action and penalties
