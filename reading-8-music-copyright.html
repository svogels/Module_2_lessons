Why Music Copyright is Complex

‚ö†Ô∏è Here's the complexity: When you listen to a song, you're actually experiencing TWO separate copyrighted works:

  1. The Musical Work (Composition) - The melody, lyrics, and arrangement
    • Owned by: Songwriter/composer (or their publisher)
  2. The Sound Recording (Master) - The specific recorded performance
    • Owned by: Recording artist and/or record label

Why it matters: You often need permission from BOTH copyright owners to use music legally!

üìå Example: "Yesterday" by The Beatles

  • Musical Work: Written by Paul McCartney ‚Üí Publishing rights owned by Sony/ATV Music Publishing
  • Sound Recording: The Beatles' 1965 recording ‚Üí Master recording owned by Apple Corps/Universal Music

To use The Beatles' recording of "Yesterday" in your video, you need permission from BOTH Sony/ATV (for the song) AND Universal Music (for that specific recording).

But you could record your own version and only need permission from Sony/ATV for the song!

Types of Music Rights

üéº Performance Rights

What: The right to perform music publicly

Includes:

  • Live performances
  • Radio/TV broadcasts
  • Streaming services
  • Background music in businesses
  • Music at events/venues

Who collects: APRA (Australasian Performing Right Association)

üéôÔ∏è Mechanical Rights

What: The right to reproduce and distribute

Includes:

  • Making CDs/vinyl
  • Digital downloads
  • Streaming (reproduction aspect)
  • Recording a cover version

Who collects: AMCOS (Australasian Mechanical Copyright Owners Society)

üì∫ Synchronisation Rights (Sync)

What: The right to pair music with visual media

Includes:

  • Movies and TV shows
  • Advertisements
  • YouTube videos
  • Video games
  • Social media content

Who grants it: Must negotiate directly with copyright owners

üíø Master Use Rights

What: The right to use a specific recording

Includes:

  • Using the original recording in media
  • Sampling (using parts of recordings)

Who grants it: Record label or recording owner (separate from sync rights!)

‚ö†Ô∏è The Sync License Double-Hit:

To use a commercial recording in your video (like a YouTube video with popular music), you typically need:

  1. Synchronisation License - From the songwriter/publisher (for the composition)
  2. Master Use License - From the record label (for that specific recording)

This is why it's so expensive and difficult! You're negotiating with two separate parties.

Music Rights Organizations in Australia

APRA AMCOS

APRA (Australasian Performing Right Association) and AMCOS (Australasian Mechanical Copyright Owners Society) work together to:

  • Collect royalties on behalf of songwriters, composers, and publishers
  • License music for public performance and reproduction
  • Distribute payments to rights holders

Common licenses from APRA AMCOS:

  • Business background music licenses (shops, restaurants)
  • Live event licenses
  • Radio and TV broadcast licenses
  • Online streaming licenses

PPCA (Phonographic Performance Company of Australia)

Represents record labels and recording artists for the sound recording copyright:

  • Collects royalties when recordings are played publicly
  • Licenses businesses to play recorded music
  • Works alongside APRA AMCOS (you often need licenses from both)

üí° The "Blanket License" Solution:

Businesses like cafes, gyms, and retail stores don't negotiate for each song individually. Instead, they purchase blanket licenses from APRA AMCOS and PPCA that cover all music in their catalogs.

Example: A cafe pays ~$500-1000/year (depending on size) to legally play background music from radio or streaming services.

Common Scenarios and What You Need

Scenario 1: Playing Music at a School Event

  • What you need: Performance license (usually covered by school's blanket license)
  • Who to check with: Your school administration - most schools have APRA AMCOS licenses

Scenario 2: Creating a YouTube Video with Music

  • Option A: Use YouTube's Audio Library (free, pre-cleared music)
  • Option B: License royalty-free music (Epidemic Sound, Artlist, AudioJungle)
  • Option C: Use music with Creative Commons licenses (check terms)
  • Option D: Get sync + master licenses (expensive, complex)

Reality check: Using popular commercial music without permission will likely result in your video being monetized by the rights holder or taken down.

Scenario 3: Live Streaming with Background Music

  • Platform coverage: Twitch, YouTube, and Facebook have some licensing agreements
  • What's safe: Music from platform-approved catalogs
  • What's risky: Playing Spotify, Apple Music, or personal music collection
  • Result of infringement: Muted audio, DMCA strikes, channel termination

Scenario 4: Recording a Cover Song

  • What you need: Mechanical license (for distribution)
  • Good news: In Australia, there's a statutory license for covers - you don't need to ask permission, just pay the set fee
  • How to: Arrange through AMCOS or use services like Loudr, Easy Song Licensing
  • Cost: Typically calculated per unit sold or stream

Legal Alternatives to Commercial Music

üí° Free and Legal Music Sources:

  1. YouTube Audio Library - Free music for video creators (some require attribution)
  2. Free Music Archive - Various licenses, filter by usage rights
  3. Incompetech (Kevin MacLeod) - Free with attribution or pay to remove attribution requirement
  4. Creative Commons Music
    • ccMixter - Remix-friendly music
    • Jamendo - Free for non-commercial use
    • SoundCloud - Filter by Creative Commons
  5. Public Domain Music - Classical music recordings (check specific recording's copyright)

Paid But Affordable Options:

  • Epidemic Sound - ~$15-25/month for unlimited music and sound effects
  • Artlist - Annual subscription (~$200/year) for unlimited downloads
  • AudioJungle - Pay per track ($5-50 depending on usage)
  • Uppbeat - Free tier with attribution, paid tier without

These services provide royalty-free licenses - you pay once and can use the music in your projects without ongoing royalties.

Key Takeaways

  1. Music has two copyrights - composition (musical work) and recording (sound recording)
  2. Different uses need different licenses - performance, mechanical, sync, master use
  3. APRA AMCOS and PPCA handle most blanket licensing in Australia
  4. Sync licenses are complex - need permission from both songwriter and recording owner
  5. Attribution ‚â† Permission - giving credit doesn't replace getting a license
  6. Educational use isn't a free pass - fair dealing is limited for music
  7. Use legal alternatives - royalty-free music, Creative Commons, YouTube Audio Library
  8. Platform agreements help - but don't cover all music or all uses

‚ö†Ô∏è Remember:

Music copyright is one of the most complex and actively enforced areas of copyright law. Automatic detection systems (like YouTube's Content ID) catch unauthorized music use immediately. Always assume you need permission unless you're certain you have the right license or the music is genuinely free to use.
