When Do You Need Permission?

The basic rule of copyright is simple: if you want to use someone else's copyrighted work, you need permission from the copyright owner. However, there are exceptions to this rule, such as fair dealing and statutory licensing schemes.

Here's a decision framework to help you determine if you need permission:

  1. Is the work protected by copyright?
    • If it's in the public domain (copyright has expired), you don't need permission
    • If it's clearly marked as openly licensed (e.g., Creative Commons), check the license terms
    • Otherwise, assume it IS protected by copyright
  2. Does your use fall under an exception?
    • Fair dealing for specific purposes (research, criticism, news, etc.)
    • Educational statutory licenses (if your school has them)
    • If yes, you may not need permission (but check the conditions)
  3. If not covered by an exception, you MUST seek permission

When in doubt, assume copyright exists. It's better to ask for permission unnecessarily than to infringe copyright by not asking when you should have.

Who Do You Ask for Permission?

Finding the copyright owner can sometimes be challenging. Here are the people or organizations you might need to contact:

  • The creator/author - Often the copyright owner unless they've transferred their rights
  • Publisher - For books, journals, magazines, music recordings
  • Production company - For films, TV shows, videos
  • Record label - For music recordings
  • Photographer or artist - For images and artworks
  • Website owner - For online content (check their Terms of Use)
  • Copyright collecting societies - Organizations that manage rights for multiple copyright owners

How to Request Permission

When requesting permission to use copyrighted material, you should provide:

  1. Identify yourself - Who you are and what organization you represent (if applicable)
  2. Describe the work - Title, author, date, ISBN/DOI if applicable
  3. Specify what you want to use - Which parts (pages, chapters, images, duration)
  4. Explain your intended use - Purpose, audience, format (print, digital, online)
  5. Indicate distribution - How many copies, how it will be distributed
  6. Acknowledge willingness to pay - If applicable, or ask about licensing fees

üí° Pro Tip: Request permission in writing (email is fine) and keep a copy of the permission granted. This documentation proves you acted lawfully if there's ever a dispute.

Alternatives to Seeking Permission

If you can't get permission, or want to avoid the hassle and potential cost, consider these alternatives:

  • Use works in the public domain - Works where copyright has expired (generally 70+ years old)
  • Use Creative Commons licensed works - Many creators offer their works with pre-approved permissions
    • Search sites like Flickr, Wikimedia Commons, Unsplash, Pixabay
    • Always check the specific CC license requirements (attribution, commercial use, etc.)
  • Use royalty-free stock resources - Websites offering images, music, and videos for free or for a fee
  • Create your own content - Take your own photos, write your own text, create your own graphics
  • Use government and educational resources - Many are freely available
  • Rely on fair dealing - If your use qualifies (remember the limitations)

üîç Remember:

Even when using Creative Commons or royalty-free materials, you must still comply with the specific license terms. Always read the license! Attribution (giving credit) is often required even for "free" resources.
