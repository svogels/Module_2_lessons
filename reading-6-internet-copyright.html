The Big Copyright Myth About the Internet

‚ùå MYTH

"If it's on the internet, it's free to use!"

"No copyright symbol means it's public domain!"

"If I found it on Google Images, I can use it!"

‚úÖ FACT

Just because something is accessible online doesn't mean it's free to use.

Copyright protection is automatic - no symbol or registration required.

Search engines just find images; they don't grant permission to use them.

Key principle: All creative works online are protected by copyright the moment they are created, unless explicitly stated otherwise. This includes:

  • Images, photographs, and graphics
  • Videos and animations
  • Music and audio recordings
  • Text, articles, and blog posts
  • Software and code
  • Website designs and layouts

‚ö†Ô∏è The Sharing Misconception:

When you post something online, you're making it accessible, not giving away your copyright. Just because someone shares a photo on social media doesn't mean you can use it for your own purposes without permission.

Downloading and Using Online Content

What You CAN Do Without Permission

  • Browse and view content online for personal use
  • Download for private study (personal research/study only)
  • Temporary caching by your browser (automatic technical process)
  • Share links to the original content (not downloading and re-uploading)
  • Use content covered by fair dealing (criticism, review, news reporting, etc.)

What You CANNOT Do Without Permission

  • Download and redistribute copyrighted material
  • Use images on your website, blog, or social media
  • Edit or modify copyrighted works
  • Use in presentations (even educational ones, beyond fair dealing limits)
  • Print and distribute downloaded content
  • Embed content from unauthorized sources
  • Upload to cloud storage for sharing with others

Special Considerations for Images

Photography and Visual Art

Photographers and artists automatically own the copyright to their work. This means:

  • The person who took the photo owns it (not the person in the photo)
  • Professional stock photo websites license their images - you can't just download and use them
  • Screenshots of websites, apps, or games contain copyrighted elements
  • Fan art based on copyrighted characters is a derivative work requiring permission

üì∏ Example: You take a photo at a concert. You own the copyright to your photo, but:

  • You may have agreed not to take photos (venue rules)
  • The performance itself is copyrighted by the artist
  • The music being performed is separately copyrighted
  • Commercial use of the photo could infringe multiple copyrights

Right-Click Saving

Just because you can right-click and save an image doesn't mean you should or legally can use it. The technical ability to download is not the same as legal permission to use.

Legal Alternatives to Copyright Infringement

1. Creative Commons Licensed Content

Creative Commons (CC) licenses allow creators to share their work with specific permissions. Look for images with CC licenses on:

  • Wikimedia Commons - millions of freely usable images
  • Flickr - filter by Creative Commons license
  • Unsplash, Pexels, Pixabay - free stock photo sites
  • Google Images - use "Usage Rights" filter (Tools ‚Üí Usage Rights ‚Üí Creative Commons licenses)

Important: Always check the specific CC license terms - some require attribution, some prohibit commercial use, some prohibit modifications.

2. Public Domain Content

Works in the public domain have no copyright restrictions:

  • Very old works (generally published before 1955 in Australia)
  • Works explicitly released to public domain
  • Government works (in some jurisdictions)

3. Royalty-Free Stock Images

These are images you can license for a one-time fee or for free:

  • Free: Unsplash, Pexels, Pixabay, Canva (free tier)
  • Paid: Shutterstock, iStock, Adobe Stock, Getty Images

4. Create Your Own Content

The safest option is always to create your own:

  • Take your own photographs
  • Create your own graphics using design tools (Canva, Adobe Express)
  • Commission original artwork from artists
  • Use AI generation tools (but check their terms of service)

üí° Pro Tip - The Attribution Formula:

When using Creative Commons content, provide proper attribution:

"[Title]" by [Author] is licensed under [License Type]. [Link to license]

Example: "Sunset Beach" by Jane Smith is licensed under CC BY 4.0. https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by/4.0/

Social Media and Copyright

What Happens When You Post?

When you upload content to social media platforms:

  • You retain copyright to your original work
  • You grant the platform a license to display, distribute, and sometimes monetize your content
  • Others don't automatically get permission to use your content just because you posted it

Reposting and Sharing

Best Practices:

  • Use the platform's share/retweet features - these are generally acceptable
  • Don't download and re-upload others' content as your own
  • Ask permission before screenshotting and posting someone's content
  • Tag the original creator when sharing (not legally sufficient but good practice)
  • Understand memes - even though widely shared, they often infringe copyright

User-Generated Content Risks

Be careful when posting content that includes:

  • Background music - even short clips can trigger copyright claims
  • TV/movie clips - even for commentary, there are limits
  • Brand logos - trademark issues in addition to copyright
  • Other people's photos - even if you're in them, the photographer owns copyright

Key Takeaways

  1. Assume everything online is copyrighted unless explicitly stated otherwise
  2. Accessibility ‚â† Permission - just because you can download it doesn't mean you can use it
  3. Google doesn't grant rights - search engines find content, they don't license it
  4. Attribution is not permission - giving credit doesn't replace getting permission
  5. Use legal alternatives - Creative Commons, public domain, royalty-free stock, or create your own
  6. When in doubt, ask - contact the copyright owner for permission
  7. Social media sharing - use platform features rather than downloading and re-uploading
