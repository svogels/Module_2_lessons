What is Fair Dealing?

Fair dealing is an important exception in Australian copyright law that allows you to use copyrighted material without permission or payment in certain circumstances. Unlike the broader "fair use" concept in US law, Australian fair dealing is more narrowly defined and applies only to specific purposes.

Fair dealing recognizes that there are legitimate reasons to use portions of copyrighted works for purposes that benefit society, such as education, research, criticism, and news reporting.

When Does Fair Dealing Apply?

Under Australian copyright law, fair dealing applies to the following specific purposes:

  • Research or study - For educational or academic purposes
  • Criticism or review - For analyzing or commenting on creative works
  • Parody or satire - For humorous or ironic commentary
  • News reporting - For journalism and current affairs
  • Legal advice - For professional legal purposes
  • Judicial proceedings - For court cases and tribunals

It's important to note that just because your use falls into one of these categories doesn't automatically make it fair dealing. You still need to consider the fairness factors.

The Four Fairness Factors

When determining whether a use qualifies as fair dealing, courts consider four key factors:

  1. The purpose and character of the use - Is it for one of the permitted purposes? Is it transformative (adding new meaning or message)?
  2. The nature of the copyrighted work - Published works are more likely to be fair dealing than unpublished works
  3. The amount and substantiality used - How much of the work did you use? Was it the "heart" of the work?
  4. The effect on the potential market - Does your use compete with or harm the market for the original work?

All four factors must be considered together. No single factor is decisive on its own.

Fair Dealing in Practice

Here are some common scenarios where fair dealing may apply:

  • Academic research: Quoting passages from books or articles in your research paper with proper attribution
  • Film reviews: Using short clips from a movie in a video review to illustrate your critical points
  • News reporting: Reproducing photographs or video footage to report on current events
  • Educational purposes: Copying pages from a textbook for classroom study (within the 10% guideline)
  • Parody videos: Creating a satirical version of a song or video for comedic commentary

Attribution Requirements

Even when using material under fair dealing, you must provide sufficient acknowledgment of the source. This typically includes:

  • The title of the work
  • The author or creator's name
  • Where it was published (if applicable)
