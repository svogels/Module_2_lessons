Copyright in Educational Settings

Educational institutions have special provisions under Australian copyright law that allow limited copying and use of copyrighted materials for teaching and learning. However, these provisions come with specific rules and limitations that must be followed.

Understanding copyright in education is essential because teachers and students regularly interact with copyrighted materials - textbooks, articles, videos, images, and online resources.

Statutory Licensing Schemes

Australian educational institutions can benefit from statutory licensing schemes that allow them to copy and communicate copyrighted materials under certain conditions. These schemes are managed by copyright collecting societies like Copyright Agency.

  • Copyright Agency - Manages licenses for text and images
  • Screenrights - Manages licenses for TV and radio broadcasts
  • Schools pay licensing fees that are distributed to copyright owners
  • These schemes simplify the process of obtaining permission for educational copying

Under these schemes, educational institutions can make copies of works without seeking individual permission from each copyright owner, as long as they follow the rules and pay the required fees.

The 10% Rule

One of the most important limitations in educational copying is the 10% rule (also known as the "one chapter or one article" rule):

The 10% Rule for Educational Copying:

  • You can copy 10% of the pages in a book (if it has 10 or more pages)
  • OR one chapter from a book
  • OR one article from a periodical (magazine, journal, newspaper)
  • Whichever results in the greater number of pages

Example: If a textbook has 200 pages and you want to copy for educational purposes, you can copy up to 20 pages (10%). But if one chapter is 30 pages, you can copy the entire 30-page chapter.

Fair Dealing for Research and Study

Students and researchers can rely on the fair dealing exception for research or study. This allows copying of a "reasonable portion" of a work without permission.

  • For research or study, a "reasonable portion" follows the 10% or one chapter rule
  • Must be for genuine research or study purposes (not commercial)
  • Must include sufficient acknowledgment of the source
  • Cannot be used to avoid purchasing the original work

Digital Materials and Online Learning

With the increase in online and digital learning, copyright considerations extend to:

  • Learning Management Systems (LMS) - Materials uploaded to platforms like Canvas or Moodle
  • Digital copies - Scanning physical materials to create digital versions
  • Online streaming - Showing videos or playing audio in virtual classrooms
  • Screen sharing - Displaying copyrighted content during online lessons

The same rules apply to digital copies as physical copies - you must still respect the 10% rule and statutory licensing conditions. Just because something is done digitally doesn't mean copyright doesn't apply.
